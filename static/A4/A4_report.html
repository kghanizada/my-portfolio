Dataset

The dataset titled 'Annual Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Air Emissions Accounts' compiles annual and quarterly greenhouse gas emissions data related to economic activities across various regions, spanning the years 2010 to 2022. It provides information on greenhouse gas emissions in million metric tons of CO2 equivalent from various countries or regions and worldwide. The dataset utilizes annual source data from the System of Environmental-Economic Accounts (SEEA), the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) National Inventory submissions, the Emissions Database for Global Atmospheric Research (EDGAR), and Climate Watch, along with sub-annual data from the International Energy Agency (IEA) and other economic datasets. This comprehensive dataset aids in analyzing the impact of economic activities on GHG emissions.

Proposition

  1. Global Greenhouse Gas (GHG) air emissions experienced a notable decline in 2020, primarily due to the unprecedented global response to the COVID-19 pandemic, which included lockdowns and a temporary halt in many economic activities.

Reflection

Reflecting on the design process for both of these visualizations showed me the complex interplay between the objective presentation of data and the subjective art of storytelling through data and data visualization. Since I had some experience in Tableau and data exploration from the previous assignments, it was straightforward to explore the data through several initial analysis and visualizations. There were some challenges with data transformation and visualizations such as learning how to pivot the data since I chose to use the climate change dataset as well as using a calculated data field. Additionally, the challenge and nuance lay in the subtleties of design decisions that communicate a narrative, highlight specific data points, or contextualize yearly data within longer trends.

The first visualization, intended to emphasize the significance of the 2020 GHG emissions drop, was about harnessing the power of color, annotations, and stark contrasts to draw attention to a pivotal moment. The second visualization's goal was the antithesis, employing averages and long-term trends to downplay the same event. This duality underscored the dichotomy that exists within data visualization—where every choice has the potential to persuade or dissuade, to reveal or to obscure. What was surprising was how even the most earnest decisions, aimed at clarity, could inadvertently carry persuasive weight. For example, the use of a calculated field for averaging emissions was an earnest attempt at simplification, but it also smoothed out variances that could have been pivotal to a different narrative.

“Ethical analysis and visualization” is a commitment to transparency, context, and intent. Ethical visualizations should provide the viewer with the necessary context to understand the data and the narrative without resorting to distortion. The designers of the visualizations should strive to be transparent about the methodologies and transformations applied to the data. This process has highlighted that "acceptable" persuasive choices are those that aim to clarify, not confuse; to inform, not deceive. They should invite scrutiny and stand robust against it.

Distinguishing “acceptable” persuasive choices from “misleading” ones boils down to the intent behind the visualization and the effect it has on the viewer’s understanding. Acceptable persuasion guides the viewer to insights drawn from honest data representations; misleading ones manipulate perception to preordained conclusions, possibly at the expense of the truth. The bounds here are subjective and closely tied to the data visualizer's moral compass—how they interpret their role either as a neutral reporter or as an advocate with a message. In either case, the power of visualized data should be handled with respect and transparency for its potential impact.
